Ocean Exploration

About the Ocean

The ocean covers 70 percent of Earth's surface. It contains about 1.35 billion cubic kilometers (324 million cubic miles) of water, which is about 97 percent of all the water on Earth. The ocean makes all life on Earth possible, and makes the planet appear blue when viewed from space. Earth is the only planet in our solar system that is definitely known to contain liquid water. Although the ocean is one continuous body of water, oceanographers have divided it into five principal areas: the Pacific, Atlantic, Indian, Arctic, and Southern Oceans. The Atlantic, Indian, and Pacific Oceans merge into icy waters around Antarctica.

First Deep Sea Exploration

1864 Norwegian Research (the spark)

The first deep-sea life forms were discovered in 1864 when Norwegian researchers Michael Sars and Georg Ossian Sars obtained a sample of a stalked crinoid at a depth of 3,109 m (10,200 ft).

From 1872 to 1876, a landmark ocean study was carried out by British scientists aboard HMS Challenger, a screw corvette that was converted into a survey ship in 1872. The Challenger expedition covered 127,653 kilometres (68,927 nmi), and shipboard scientists collected hundreds of samples and hydrographic measurements, discovering more than 4,700 new species of marine life, including deep-sea organisms.They are also credited with providing the first real view of major seafloor features such as the deep ocean basins.

Technology in Deep Sea

From simple to complex and so on...

Ocean exploration began with shockingly simple tools. For centuries, the lead line, a simple weighted rope marked with intervals, was the only way to measure depth. Navigators used the sextant and chronometer to pinpoint their location on the vast, empty sea. A huge leap came with sampling tools like the dredge, a heavy metal net dragged along the seafloor. The 1870s H.M.S. Challenger expedition used this to discover thousands of new species, proving the deep sea was not a dead zone. To sample the water itself, they invented the Nansen bottle, a clever tube that could snap shut at a specific depth to trap a sample.

The 20th century brought two game-changers: SONAR and diving. SONAR (Sound Navigation and Ranging) uses sound "pings" to map the seafloor from a ship. It created the first maps of the hidden underwater world, revealing vast mountain ranges (like the Mid-Ocean Ridge) and deep trenches. At the same time, SCUBA, co-invented by Jacques Cousteau, untethered humans from the surface, allowing true exploration of coastal ecosystems. For the extreme deep, manned submersibles (HOVs) were built. After early attempts like the cabled Bathysphere, the Bathyscaphe Trieste famously reached the bottom of the Mariana Trench in 1960. Modern subs like DSV Alvin became true workhorses, letting scientists directly observe and sample deep-sea life, like hydrothermal vents.

Today, exploration is dominated by advanced robotics. ROVs (Remotely Operated Vehicles) are tethered robots "flown" by pilots on the ship. With HD cameras and robotic arms, they do precise work, from studying vents to examining shipwrecks like the Titanic. AUVs (Autonomous Underwater Vehicles), however, are completely untethered. These pre-programmed robots "mow the lawn" to gather mapping data over huge areas. Finally, satellite oceanography lets us scan the entire global ocean surface, tracking sea-surface temperature, height, and even plankton blooms from space.

Discoveries

The top Ocean exploration discoveries

Ocean exploration has completely changed how we see our planet. For centuries, most people assumed the deep ocean was a cold, dark, and lifeless void. This idea was proven wrong by early voyages like the H.M.S. Challenger expedition (1870s), which dredged up thousands of unknown species from miles deep, proving the deep sea was full of bizarre and varied life.

But the most stunning discovery came in 1977 with hydrothermal vents. Scientists found superheated, mineral-rich water gushing from the seafloor, like underwater geysers. Around these toxic vents, they found a dense ecosystem of giant tube worms, crabs, and shrimp, all thriving in total darkness. This was revolutionary because it was the first discovery of life that doesn't depend on the sun. Instead of photosynthesis, these creatures use chemosynthesis, getting energy from the chemicals in the vent water.

Mapping the seafloor also revealed the Mid-Ocean Ridge, a massive underwater mountain range that wraps around the entire globe like the seam on a baseball. This discovery was the key piece of evidence for plate tectonics, showing exactly where the Earth's crust is spreading apart.

More recently, exploration has shown that bioluminescence (animals making their own light) isn't just a rare curiosity; it's the primary language of the deep ocean, used for everything from hunting to hiding. And, of course, exploration has also located our own lost history, like the famous discovery of the RMS Titanic, and has allowed humans to visit the deepest point on Earth, the Challenger Deep, nearly seven miles down.
